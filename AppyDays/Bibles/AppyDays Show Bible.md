# AppyDays Show Bible

`AppyDays` is a startup melodrama about a company too small to deserve its own mythology and too ambitious to stop inventing one.

It sells connection, workflow, scale, and modernity.
Internally, it runs on hunches, favors, panic, unfinished systems, and people who are only barely choosing to still be here.

The company is not yet a real institution.
That is the source of most of the comedy and most of the damage.

## Core Premise

AppyDays is a tiny app company operating out of a larger coworking space.

The company keeps attracting problems scaled for a much bigger organization:

- investor expectations
- client demands
- hiring theater
- product promises
- office politics
- reputational risk

The central contradiction is simple:

- half the room thinks they are too small for this
- half the room thinks they are not thinking big enough
- the rest want everyone to shut up and let them work

That mismatch is the engine.

## What The Company Actually Is

AppyDays is not a sleek, late-stage startup.
It is a fragile founder-led company with fewer than ten real insiders.

It lives inside a coworking environment, which means its dysfunction is never fully private.

That matters because the show is not just about internal startup problems.
It is also about adjacency:

- investors drifting through
- clients arriving too early
- candidates overhearing the wrong thing
- neighboring tenants witnessing the mess
- building staff enforcing boundaries at the worst possible time

The company keeps trying to project scale before it has earned structure.

## Tone

Prestige startup farce.

The language is corporate, but the emotional texture is intimate, petty, scared, aspirational, and overexposed.

People talk like they are:

- in a status meeting
- in a breakup
- in a funding pitch
- trying not to admit they are improvising

The show should never feel like generic office satire.
It should feel like premature organizational identity:
people speaking as if they already belong to the company they are still trying to invent.

## Series Engine

Each episode starts with an incoming pressure:

- investor expectation
- client demand
- product failure
- hiring or candidate issue
- coworking-space conflict
- reputation problem
- internal promise coming due

That pressure hits a company that is too small to absorb it cleanly.

The episode then runs through the same human geometry:

1. Someone tries to narrate the problem upward into opportunity.
2. Someone tries to translate it downward into actual work.
3. Someone points out the physical, technical, or logistical limit.
4. Someone treats the emotional or political problem as if it were merely procedural.
5. The room survives by making a commitment, tradeoff, lie, or framing move that creates the next problem.

AppyDays does not resolve cleanly.
It converts one unstable arrangement into another.

## Status Quo Rule

At the end of an AppyDays episode, the company is still alive, but the terms of staying alive have changed.

That change should usually take one of these forms:

- a promise has been made
- a boundary has been crossed
- a dependency has been exposed
- a role has silently changed
- somebody has lost cover
- somebody has become indispensable
- the company now owes someone something it cannot easily repay

## Environment

The coworking space matters because it externalizes startup shame.

AppyDays is never fully alone.
Its private dysfunction is always at risk of becoming:

- a hallway rumor
- a scheduling issue
- a leasing issue
- a candidate impression
- an investor concern
- a facilities problem

That gives the show a built-in audience and a built-in pressure valve.

## Set Logic

AppyDays does not need many locations.
It needs a small number of social-pressure rooms.

Target core set list:

- Suite A
  primary founder room; planning, bluffing, posturing, closed-door conflict
- Suite B
  work room; shipping pressure, technical truth, handoff fights, exhaustion
- Common Area
  overheard conflict, fake casualness, coworking ecology, investor or client drift
- Hallway
  interception, damage control, candidate exposure, private remarks becoming semi-public
- Reception
  access control, embarrassment, first impressions, scheduling, building authority

Optional secondary spaces if needed later:

- conference room
- kitchenette
- outside loading or smoking area

Weak spaces should be avoided unless they have a strong dramatic job.
The show does not need random rooms just because actors fit in them.

## Company Structure

Keep the real company small.

Ideal inner circle:

- two founders
- two to four operational employees
- one junior or recently absorbed person
- one external but recurring investor pressure source

Everyone else should mostly belong to the surrounding ecology, not the company itself.

## Founders

### Clive

Business founder.

Clive needs a system.
He does not build one himself.
He pushes until somebody else turns instinct into process.

He is not just generic finance.
He is the person who recognizes when fantasy is becoming cost, exposure, or humiliation.
He gets harsh about real things.
He forces definition.

He believes adulthood consists of finally naming what something costs.

### Noah

Technical-business founder.

Noah is not the pure technical genius.
He is the hunch-driven operator-builder who can make a product exist, mostly because he has been building things since he was a kid and has not stopped.

His business sense is also instinctive.
He understands product shape, workflow shape, and how reality eventually asserts itself.

He and Clive work because:

- Clive demands systems
- Noah builds them when cornered or bored

Noah does not want to be mythologized.
He wants one conversation per week to occur in shared reality.

## Core Employees

### Sal

Best engineer in the company.
Possibly better than Noah in raw technical depth.

Noah knows this and keeps him close for exactly that reason.

Sal is the material reality of the product.
He knows what is actually broken, what is survivable, and what everyone is pretending not to understand.

### Felicity

Brought in through Noah.
Important rule: there is no creep energy in the premise.

Noah liking Felicity is background human texture, not workplace coercion.
He got her the job because he thought she would be good and because he wanted her around, then chose not to cash in emotionally on that access.

The interesting dramatic residue is:

- asymmetry of origin
- professionalism under private awareness
- eventual moving on

She is not there to validate Noah.
She is there because the company needs someone to force impossible people into timelines.

### Ivy

Junior developer or recently hired former intern.

She is partly there because Noah is there.
That does not make her passive.
It makes her structurally tied to the founder who still seems closest to reality.

She is useful because she still sees which rituals are fake.

## External Orbit

### Gideon

Investor, not operator.

He should feel more like a face, believer, or angel-investor-type pressure source than the actual engine of the company.

He represents narrative escalation:

- bigger framing
- bigger expectations
- more abstraction
- more pressure to act like a company that already exists at scale

### Janine

Better fit as coworking or building management than internal HR.

She should manage order, access, scheduling, lease boundaries, and interpersonal fallout for the larger space.

That makes her dramatically stronger.
She is not a startup employee trying to police coworkers.
She is an external authority whose politeness can still close doors.

### Marcy, Walt, Donovan

Keep them tied to the space, facilities, security, maintenance, or building operations rather than to the startup itself.

That preserves the company's small size while giving the world recurring witnesses and pressure agents.

## Character Clusters

The show runs best when the cast falls into recurring pressure roles.

### Narrative / Expansion

- Clive
- Gideon

These characters make the company sound bigger, more urgent, or more inevitable than it really is.

### Systems / Reality

- Noah
- Sal

These characters know what has to exist materially for the story to stop being fiction.

### Coordination / Survival

- Felicity
- Janine

These characters turn chaos into process, access, boundaries, ownership, and damage control.

### Witness / Exposure

- Ivy
- space-adjacent outsiders

These characters make it harder for everyone else to pretend the mess is invisible.

## What Scenes Are Usually About

AppyDays scenes should usually be about one visible business issue hiding one deeper human issue.

Common visible issues:

- a product demo
- a broken feature
- a client ask
- a candidate visit
- an investor drop-in
- a branding or messaging dispute
- an office-use or space-use conflict
- an impossible delivery date
- a hiring or firing question

Common hidden issues:

- status panic
- debt fear
- role insecurity
- founder resentment
- class embarrassment
- competence envy
- loneliness disguised as professionalism
- dependency nobody wants to name

## What Makes AppyDays Distinct

AppyDays is not about "tech people are annoying."

It is about a tiny company trying to behave like a real historical force before it has enough payroll, product stability, or emotional maturity to support that self-image.

Its dramatic language is:

- buzzwords as threats
- process as intimacy
- admin details as crisis triggers
- strategy as cover for shame
- competence as something that costs someone

## What Resolution Looks Like

The room usually resolves by choosing one of these:

- ship something risky
- promise something impossible
- hide a truth temporarily
- make somebody own a mess they did not create
- let the building or schedule absorb the fallout
- accept help that changes the power balance

The key is that the episode ends with the company still functioning, but less innocently.

## Short Version

`AppyDays` is a startup serial about a tiny app company in a coworking space where every crisis is too big for the headcount, every room is slightly too public, and everyone keeps trying to talk like a proper organization before one actually exists.
