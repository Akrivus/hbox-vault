# AppyDays Roster Compilation

This note answers the problem behind "rewrite more than seven prompts":

If `AppyDays` is a tiny founder-led company inside a coworking space, then every recurring actor needs a consistent relationship to:

- the company
- the space
- the startup myth
- the outside world

Otherwise the cast starts behaving like one big generic office ensemble.

## First Principle

Not everyone in the `AppyDays` roster should think they work for AppyDays.

The cast should be split into layers:

1. core company
2. company orbit
3. coworking/building ecology
4. outside world pressure
5. event-specific uniforms or incident roles

That keeps the show focused while still letting it expand.

## Core Company

These are the people who should treat AppyDays as their main institutional home.

| Character | Best fit | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Clive | co-founder, business | Core founder pressure source; translates fantasy into cost and decision |
| Noah | co-founder, product/technical systems | Core founder-builder; translates hunch into product and process |
| Sal | senior engineer / infra / systems | Material reality check; indispensable technical ballast |
| Felicity | project or operations lead | Converts chaos into ownership, timelines, follow-up, and delivery pressure |
| Ivy | junior developer or QA-dev hybrid | Recently absorbed into the company's reality; witness to fake rituals and broken handoffs |

This group should usually be fewer than six people.

## Company Orbit

These people recur around the company but should not all feel like everyday employees.

They can appear in scenes about funding, clients, narrative, brand, hiring, or the wider office ecosystem.

| Character | Best fit | Why |

| Marcus | contractor, growth advisor, or comms consultant | Useful when the scene is about pitch, optics, or messaging warfare |

These characters should not all appear with the same default authority.
They are tools for specific pressure types.

## Coworking / Building Ecology

These people belong to the environment more than the company.

That is good.

It means the company's dysfunction is never fully private.

| Character | Best fit | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Janine | coworking operations / tenant relations / building administration | Soft-power boundary enforcer; turns startup mess into lease and access issues |
| Walt | facility manager | Represents infrastructure, tickets, access, and physical reality |
| Ricky | on-site security | Reframes awkwardness and chaos as incidents, threats, or policy problems |
| Donovan | building security / overnight supervisor / risk-sensitive operations | More severe version of security pressure; useful when conflict turns unnerving |
| Marcy | custodian / after-hours witness / building memory | Sees what people leave behind and punctures executive self-mythologizing |

This layer is one of the best reasons AppyDays can sustain itself as a show.

These people:

- witness the startup's behavior
- impose limits
- become accidental confidants
- turn private panic into a public-enough problem

## Outside World Pressure

These are not always named as separate permanent characters.
Sometimes they can be generic or costumed roles.

Typical outside functions:

- client
- candidate
- investor guest
- neighboring founder
- landlord representative
- journalist
- vendor
- family member
- emergency responder

The key is that they should arrive with a job.
They do not exist to make the world feel "full."
They exist to stress the company's weak points.

The question is:

What recurring dynamic do they activate?

This is what "compiling" the cast should mean:

Every extra should have a stable insertion logic, not just a costume and a speaking style.

## Uniformed Extras

Adding costumed extras like `Fireman`, `Firewoman`, `Policeman`, `Policewoman` can work, but only if they are treated as pressure roles, not ambient population.

They should not exist to simulate a whole city.

They should exist because a startup's tiny private dysfunction has spilled far enough outward that formal authority has entered the frame.

## Best Rule For Uniformed Extras

A uniformed extra should appear only when one of these becomes true:

- the company has created a physical incident
- the building must respond officially
- an outsider has escalated beyond coworking staff
- legal/liability stakes have become visible
- a scene needs the humiliation of formal public consequence

That means these roles are not "background realism."
They are escalation markers.

## Suggested Uniformed Roles

| Role | Best use |
| --- | --- |
| Fireman / Firewoman | alarm, sprinklers, overheating server rack, kitchen mishap, forced evacuation, building drill gone real |
| Policeman / Policewoman | trespass dispute, stalking complaint, theft report, domestic spill into office, public scene too visible to bury |
| EMT / Paramedic | panic attack, injury, dehydration, stimulant crash, sleep-deprivation collapse |
| Inspector / Fire Marshal | compliance embarrassment, occupancy breach, unsafe setup, event gone too large |
| Courier / Process Server | legal pressure, investor paperwork, debt, lawsuit, exposure arriving physically |

These roles should usually:

- appear briefly
- force a frame change
- embarrass the company
- clarify who actually has authority

## How To Avoid "Nothing Forever"

The danger is not having extras.
The danger is letting every role become equivalent ambient chatter.

To stay focused:

### 1. Keep the institution small

The real company should remain tiny.

### 2. Give outsiders scene jobs

Every non-core character should enter to do one of a few things:

- force access
- impose a boundary
- expose a lie
- witness a humiliation
- demand a commitment
- reframe the room's status

### 3. Preserve rank difference

A founder, investor, building manager, custodian, security guard, and candidate should not all sound like peers.

### 4. Use ecology, not randomness

The coworking world is a surrounding pressure field, not a sandbox for random NPC socializing.

### 5. End with changed obligations

Scenes should not just spin.
They should change:

- who owes whom
- who has access
- who now knows
- who got embarrassed
- who must act next

## AppyDays Role Schema

This is a good default schema for future prompt compilation:

| Layer | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Founder | narrates scale, demands structure, commits the company |
| Builder | knows what can actually exist materially |
| Coordinator | turns conflict into ownership and timelines |
| Witness | documents breakage and punctures denial |
| Investor / Narrative pressure | makes belief and optics more expensive |
| Building authority | enforces limits, access, dignity, and consequence |
| Service worker / maintenance witness | sees through institutional theater |
| Uniformed authority | marks escalation into formal public consequence |

When adding a new actor, assign them a layer before writing their style.

## Practical Rewrite Implication

The prompt rewrite should not just "update seven main characters."

It should:

1. classify every existing AppyDays actor into one of these layers
2. rewrite prompts so their institutional relationship is obvious
3. stop treating the whole roster like coworkers
4. reserve generic-costume roles for official consequence, not ambient filler
